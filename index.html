Fossil Fuel Pricing in Sub-Saharan Africa

While fossil fuels receive significant explicit and implicit subsidies globally, they remain an inefficient mechanism for social support (IMF 2025). This dashboard tracks total subsidies (explicit plus implicit) in Sub-Saharan African countries as they evolve with international oil prices. It underscores the opportunity for governments to capture fiscal savings by replacing broad fuel subsidies with better-targeted policies that directly support those in need.

Explicit subsidy

Explicit (or fiscal) subsidies occur when the price paid by consumers is below the supply cost—the resource cost, such as labor, capital and raw materials, of producing or importing the fuel. However, from an economic perspective, these subsidies fail to capture extenral costs of fossil fuel use, including the social cost of local air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions.

Implicit subsidy

Implicit subsidies include environmental externalities, where prices fail to reach a socially efficient level that accounts for global climate damage, health costs from air pollution, and road-related costs like congestion and accidents.
